Ingredients 6 questions

The gut-brain axis is the two-way communication network between your digestive system and your brain — running through the vagus nerve, gut hormones, and microbiome-produced neurotransmitters. Your gut influences your mood, energy, cravings, and stress response far more than most people realise.

95% of your serotonin is produced in your gut. Snacks formulated with prebiotic fiber feed the beneficial bacteria linked to calmer craving signals. That's the idea behind everything we make.

Read Gut-Brain 101 →

We use KSM-66 Ashwagandha — the most clinically studied form. At relevant doses, it has been linked to reduced cortisol levels and support for the HPA axis (the system that manages your stress response). Lower cortisol means quieter stress-driven cravings.

We use it because the science supports it at the dose we include — not as a marketing ingredient sprinkled in for the label.

We use monk fruit — zero glycemic impact, no digestive side effects, and it doesn't disrupt your gut microbiome. No erythritol, no maltitol, no xylitol.

Sugar alcohols are in almost every "healthy" snack bar. They can cause bloating and — more importantly — may disrupt the beneficial gut bacteria we're trying to feed. We don't use them. Simple.

Prebiotic fiber — we use chicory root — specifically feeds the beneficial bacteria in your gut microbiome. Unlike regular fiber that just adds bulk, prebiotic fiber is selectively fermented by good bacteria, producing short-chain fatty acids that may help regulate hunger hormones and support gut lining integrity.

Each bar contains over 4g — a meaningful amount, not a trace.

Amla (Indian gooseberry) is one of the most antioxidant-dense foods on earth — used for centuries in Ayurvedic medicine. It has been linked to reduced inflammation, improved gut microbiome diversity, and blood sugar stabilisation. We include it in the Peanut Butter Bar as a functional adaptogen, not a marketing claim.

None. Zero artificial colours, flavours, sweeteners, or preservatives. Every ingredient on the label is there because it contributes something real — either to taste, texture, or function. If it doesn't earn its place, it doesn't go in.
